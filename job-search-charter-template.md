# Template

The template below is copied from Phyl Terry's Never Search Alone. It references a "JSC" or Job Search Council of friendly humans who you can partner with on this exercise. I absolutely recommend doing a first, human-only pass!
Where I think AI is really helpful is highlighting areas of self-awareness and blind spots. Upload a first draft of this document, your resume, plus lots of artifacts from your past few years of work. These should be things that you are proud of and/or directly address your job performance (peer reviews, performance reviews, etc.)

**Prompt**: My goal with this space is to identify jobs that might be a good fit for me to apply to. Before I search for jobs, I want to use you as a partner to refine what I'm looking for from a job at a high level. I've uploaded [XYZ] documents to help us get started:

- _Describe your documents_

Can you review each of these files, and then kick things off by acting as my thought partner? I want you to be deeply engaged and even challenging in this discussion. You should push me to defend my assumptions and help me qualify how important certain aspects of the job or company are to me. It's also important for you to know that [fill in any job search or personal considerations here.]

## Contents

1. What you love and hate doing
2. Your “must-haves”
3. Your “must-nots”
4. Your career goals (short-term and long-term)

### 1. What you love and hate doing

Here again is the exercise in the book to help you determine what you love and hate doing.

1. **Pair up with a member of your JSC.**
2. **Interview each other about what you hate doing.**
   When you are paired up, interview each other about all the kinds of jobs, industries, functions, etc. that you hate and things you do not love doing. What about past jobs did you dislike? Why? Your partner should take notes on what you don’t like doing. Then, in the next steps, you will get the copy of their notes to review it.

3. **Review your list separately.**
   Get your list of what you hate doing from your partner in this exercise. Each of you spend 15 minutes reviewing your own lists and adding anything.

4. **Invert focus to what you love doing.**
   Now each of you invert your own lists. If you put down that you hate business travel, then invert that to you love jobs with little travel (and try to get more specific — maybe, you love running teams in one location, rather than national or global teams). Keep in mind that if you hate doing something, you will not always necessarily like or love doing the inverse. You may just be satisfied with the absence of the thing.

In this exercise, be as open and honest as you can. Keep in mind that you can be good at something but not love it. For example, I’m quite good at finance, but coming out of Harvard Business School, I made a very clear choice not to go to Wall Street. I did not like the typical 1990s macho culture of those firms.

Also, look for opportunities to be creative.

Let’s say you love to travel but do not like business travel. Could you find a way to combine those two things and improve your work/life balance in a way that you love your job more?

Once you have done this exercise, now move to the “Must-Nots.” This is a related but different exercise. There is some overlap, but here, you are going the next level down. Maybe you know that you love media and would love to work for a streaming company in a marketing role that requires little travel.

### 2: The Must-Nots Exercise

1. **Pair up with a different member of your JSC.**

2. **Interview each other about what you do not want.**
   When you are paired up, interview each other about all the things you do not want in your next job. For example, you might say that you don’t want to manage more than 10 people, you don’t want a boss who micromanages, etc. 
3. **Use what I call the “bad bosses” prompt.**
   If you are having any trouble getting your partner to articulate “must-nots,” then ask them again about past roles — “Have you ever had a bad job or a bad boss? If yes, what did that teach you about what you do not want in a boss, culture, etc.?”
4. **Review your lists with each other.**

The irony of inverting and looking at the negative is that it’s a positive and fun experience.

### 3: Your Must-Haves

Most job seekers tend _not_ to think about their Must-Haves, especially if they are out of work.

That’s a mistake. You’ll want to go through the process of determining your must-haves.

So, with the same partner from the second exercise, run through these steps.

1. **Review your Must-Nots.**
   Begin this exercise by reviewing your partner’s Must-Nots from the last exercise. Again, these can be a road map to your must-haves.

2. **Reverse the Must-Nots into Must-Haves.**
   Then help your partner invert their Must-Nots into potential Must-Haves. For example, if a Must-Not is “frequent travel,” then a Must-Have would be something like “travel no more than once a month.”

3. **Each separately refine your Must-Haves.**
   Then you should each review the first pass on your Must-Haves that your partner created for you, and make any edits or changes.

4. **Present your Must-Haves for Discussion.**
   Then return to your partner and present your Must-Haves for discussion and feedback. Discuss each item. Ask what might be missing or should be edited.

### 4: Your Career Goals

Look over the results of the three exercises above, and then sit down and ask yourself, “What kind of job do I want now and in the long run?”

Do you want to get promoted from Director of Product to VP of Product?

Do you want to become a Chief Operating Officer or a CEO?

Whatever the answers are, write down both your short-term career goal (i.e., what kind of job do you want next) and your long-term career goal (i.e., what you want eventually).

Don’t worry if you are not sure what your short- or long-term goals are.

Remember, this is a _draft_.

Your Listening Tour will help you not only understand the market, but also understand _yourself_ and what you want.
